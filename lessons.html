Lesson-plan guide

Building a Lesson Around a Breakout

A ready structure for slotting a Critical Thinking Online Breakout into a full lesson, following a standard Texas lesson-plan format. Use the outline as-is or copy the field list into your campus template. A worked example for every grade appears at the end.

🧑‍🏫 How to use this guide: a breakout works best as the engage or collaborative-practice phase of a lesson — not the whole lesson. The sections below mirror a standard lesson plan; each names what to decide and how the breakout fits. Pair it with the UDL and ELPS supports pages for the differentiation section.

Lesson-plan sections

1 · Basic information

Record the essentials so the plan is reusable and reviewable: teacher, campus/district, grade level, course/subject, unit, lesson title, date, duration, instructional setting (whole group / small group / centers / lab / online / blended), number of students, and prerequisite skills or prior learning. A single breakout typically fits a 20–40 minute block.

2 · Standards & alignment

Name the primary standard the breakout targets and pull the exact citation from the standards correlation guide. Add the ELPS for your emergent bilingual students (listening, speaking, reading, writing) and a language objective. List any crosswalk standards your campus requires.

3 · Lesson purpose

State the big idea / concept the breakout reveals, one or two essential questions, and a short rationale — why this matters and how it connects to the unit or the real world. The worked examples below give each grade’s big idea and essential question.

4 · Objectives

Write measurable objectives: a content objective (“Students will…”), a language objective if applicable, and success criteria students can check themselves against (“I know I am successful when I can…”). Opening all four locks is a natural, visible success marker.

5 · Vocabulary & academic language

Pre-teach the two or three terms the breakout hinges on (see each grade’s key vocabulary below). Provide student-friendly definitions, a visual, and sentence stems for discussion — for example, “This clue tells me ___, so the lock is ___.”

6 · Materials & resources

Technology: one device per student or pair, a modern browser, and the breakout link — no login and no data collected. Optional: a projector to model the first lock, headphones for read-aloud, and printed clue cards for offline groups.

7 · Assessment plan

Before (activator): a quick prompt or image to surface prior knowledge and misconceptions. During (formative): circulate and listen — which clue are pairs using, where do they stall, how do they handle the decoy? The “locks open” count is a live progress check. After (product): an exit task — students explain in one or two sentences how they cracked one lock, or answer a transfer question.

8 · Differentiation & UDL / ELPS supports

Plan universal supports first: engagement (choice of language and partner, playful retry), representation (seven languages, chunked clue cards, key vocabulary), and action & expression (four different response modes). See the UDL supports and ELPS supports pages for specific moves by principle and proficiency level.

9 · Procedure

A workable arc that places the breakout at the center — adjust the timing to your block:
  • Opening / Engage (5 min). Pose the essential question with an image or quick prompt. Preview the vocabulary and show the 🌐 language menu. Name the goal: open all four locks using the clues.
  • Model one lock (5 min). Project the breakout and think aloud through the first clue and one lock. Show how to reopen clues and how “Start over” works. Make the decoy idea explicit: a clue can be true and still not help.
  • Collaborative practice (15–20 min). Two students per device. Partners read each clue, decide which lock it serves, and justify their choice out loud before answering. Circulate and ask “which clue, which lock, why.” This is the formative window.
  • Apply & extend (5–10 min). Students replay in a second language, try a related breakout, or answer a short transfer prompt on their own.
  • Closure (5 min). Students answer: What did you learn? How does it connect to what you knew? Where else could you use this idea? Debrief the decoy as a class.

10 · Questioning plan

  • Recall: What does this clue say? Which lock does it match?
  • Reasoning: Why does this clue open that lock and not another?
  • Transfer: Where else would this idea be true — somewhere in your own online life?
  • Misconceptions: Why is the decoy clue true but not helpful here?

11 · Teacher reflection

  • What worked, and what evidence showed students learned?
  • Who needs reteaching, and who is ready for extension?
  • Which clue or lock caused the most productive struggle?
  • What will you change next time?

Worked examples — every activity

Drop-in big ideas, essential questions, and objectives for each breakout. Full standards alignment is on each activity’s teacher launch page and the correlation guide.

Grade 3 — Your First Digital Footprint (§126.8 · free)

Big ideaEverything you do online leaves a digital footprint; choosing safe links, giving credit, and being kind keep that footprint positive.
Essential questionWhat marks do you leave online, and how do you keep your footprint safe and kind?
Content objectiveStudents will describe what a digital footprint is and identify safe choices for links, credit, and kindness online.
Language objectiveStudents will use footprint, safe, and credit to explain how to stay safe and kind online, using a sentence stem.
Key vocabularydigital footprint, safe link, password, credit, kindness
Success criteriaI can explain what a digital footprint is and name one way to stay safe and one way to be kind online.

Grade 4 — The Permanent Record (§126.9 · free)

Big ideaYour online record can last a long time; good etiquette, giving creators credit, and being an upstander keep it positive.
Essential questionWhy does the internet remember, and how should you act because it does?
Content objectiveStudents will explain why online posts persist and identify etiquette, copyright, and upstander actions.
Language objectiveStudents will use permanent, etiquette, and copyright to explain responsible online behavior.
Key vocabularypermanent record, etiquette, copyright, credit, upstander
Success criteriaI can explain why posts last and name a kind, respectful, and creator-fair choice.

Grade 5 — Track the Trackers (§126.10 · free)

Big ideaA digital footprint has parts (activity, games, social); secure connections and etiquette protect it, and copyright has consequences.
Essential questionWhat makes up your footprint, and how do trackers and secure sites affect it?
Content objectiveStudents will identify components of a digital footprint, recognize a secure (HTTPS) connection, and explain copyright consequences.
Language objectiveStudents will use tracker, secure connection (HTTPS), and permission to explain how data follows them online.
Key vocabularytracker, cookies, HTTPS / secure connection, audience, permission
Success criteriaI can name parts of my footprint and explain how a secure connection and permission protect me.

Grade 6 — Fair Use Detective (§126.17 · licensed)

Big ideaIntellectual-property terms (copyright, fair use, creative commons, public domain) guide reuse; citing sources and spotting scams keep you safe.
Essential questionWhen can you reuse a creator’s work, and how do you cite it and stay safe online?
Content objectiveStudents will distinguish IP terms, cite a source, and identify phishing, malware, and identity-theft signs.
Language objectiveStudents will use fair use, public domain, and citation to explain when and how to reuse work.
Key vocabularycopyright, fair use, creative commons, public domain, citation, phishing
Success criteriaI can tell whether I may reuse a work, cite it, and spot a phishing attempt.

Grade 7 — Spot the Spin (§126.18 · licensed)

Big ideaMedia can exaggerate or misrepresent; understanding IP consequences and defending against cyberattacks builds a critical, safe user.
Essential questionHow does media spin information, and how do you defend against it and against cyberattacks?
Content objectiveStudents will evaluate how media exaggerates or misrepresents, explain IP-law consequences, and describe cyberattack defenses.
Language objectiveStudents will use spin, misrepresent, and evaluate to analyze a media message.
Key vocabularyspin, exaggerate, misrepresent, clickbait, cyberattack
Success criteriaI can spot how a message is spun and name one defense against a cyberattack.

Grade 8 — Evaluate the Source (§126.19 · licensed)

Big ideaStrong digital citizens evaluate a source’s bias and reliability, create within IP law, and respond to real cybersecurity situations.
Essential questionHow do you judge whether a digital source is reliable, and how do you create and stay secure responsibly?
Content objectiveStudents will evaluate a source’s bias and reliability, apply IP law when creating, and respond to a cybersecurity scenario.
Language objectiveStudents will use bias, reliable, and source to evaluate a website or article.
Key vocabularybias, reliability, credibility, source, breach / authentication
Success criteriaI can judge a source’s reliability and name a responsible way to create and stay secure.
